üß† Pharmacology & Mechanism

Primary Mechanism of Action

Amyl nitrite acts as a potent and rapid-acting vasodilator through the generation of nitric oxide (NO). When inhaled, it rapidly enters the bloodstream and releases NO, which activates guanylate cyclase, leading to increased cyclic GMP levels and subsequent smooth muscle relaxation in blood vessels.

Pharmacokinetics

Absorption: Rapid through lungs, immediate systemic circulation

Distribution: Quickly distributed throughout body via bloodstream

Metabolism: Rapidly hydrolyzed to amyl alcohol and nitrite

Half-life: 1-4 minutes (extremely short)

Elimination: Primarily through lungs and kidneys

Peak effect: 30 seconds to 2 minutes

Toxicity Mechanisms

Methemoglobinemia: Nitrites can oxidize hemoglobin to methemoglobin, reducing oxygen-carrying capacity

Hypotension: Severe blood pressure drops can cause fainting, falls, and cardiac events

Chemical burns: Direct contact with skin or prolonged inhalation causes tissue damage

Cardiac stress: Rapid heart rate changes can trigger arrhythmias in susceptible individuals

Medical Applications

  • Angina treatment: Historically used for immediate relief of chest pain
  • Antidote use: Emergency treatment for cyanide poisoning (methemoglobin formation)
  • Diagnostic tool: Used in certain cardiac stress tests
  • Research applications: Studying vascular function and nitric oxide pathways
